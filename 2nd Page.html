The Indigenous Perspectives of Treaty 6

You can go back to History of Treaty 6 here

We'll be starting of this page with an hypothesis made by people that work in fields that support Indigenous land ownership, which is that the Indigenous Elders, while were aware of the details of the Treaty, had a fundamentally different understanding of the treaty’s meaning, spirit, and intent [1]. There is ample evidence to support this claim, like the fact that what we consider ownership was not a widespread term or concept for the Indigenous peoples as their culture had to do more with sharing the land with others, and that in exchange of obtaining food, they keep the Land healthy for future generations, and to not take an excessive amount of resources, which contrasts with our concept of ownership. In addition to this, Treaty 9 Elders have pointed out that the written Treaty had never been fully translated into Anishinaabe or Cree, leading to a siginifact gap between what was signed and what was agreed upon.

We'll also be looking at how the Indigenous Elders viewed this Treaty, starting with that, as mentioned prior, that they understood that they were entering a partnership to share the land with the settlers, in addition to this, they were also promised what was interpreted as health care during the negotiations since there were widespread illnesses and famine, known as the "Medicine Chest" clause, but ended up being a literal Medicine Box, with support of this intepretation from the 1935 Dreaver case [2], where George Dreaver of the Mistawasis Band won a dispute against the Crown regarding the cost of Medical Supplies, making the argument that it was covered under Treaty 6 agreements, with him knowing this since he was present at the original signing of Treaty 6.

Finally, we'll be continuing the exploration of how this Treaty was viewed by the Indigenous Elders, kicking off with the understanding that the Land would still be kept healthy for practices like Hunting and Fishing, particularly on the aspect of keeping the land healthy, with a clause on providing aid during times of crisis, agricultural support by supplying farming implements, and sustainable land managemeant, meaning that the Crown would only take what was needed. Needless to say, the intepretation by the Indigenous Elders did not end up matching what actually happened as many of these promises that were both told to the elders, and were written in the Treaty, were not actually met [3], but does highlight a stark difference in the interpretation of these Treaties by the Indigenous Elders, and what actually came to fruition out of it.
